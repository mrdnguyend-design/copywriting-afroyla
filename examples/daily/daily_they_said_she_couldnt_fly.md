# DAILY | 190226 | They Said She Couldn't Fly

**Subject Line:** She walked in. They stared.
**Preview Text:** She almost didn't go in.

---

My phone rang at 9:47 PM last night.

It was Tamara. And Tamara doesn't call at 9:47 PM unless something happened.

"Girl," she said. "I have to tell you about today."

(When Tamara says "I have to tell you about today," you sit down. You pour something. You listen.)

So I sat down.

Here's the thing about Tamara — she just started at a new firm. Corporate law. The kind of office where everybody's suit looks the same and nobody's lunch smells like anything.

She'd been flat-ironing her hair every morning since the interview. Three weeks straight. Getting up an hour early. Pressing it down. Making it "neat."

But this morning — the flat iron was sitting on the counter, already plugged in — and she just... didn't pick it up.

She looked in the mirror. Her hair was OUT. Full. Big.

"I stood there for like ten minutes," she told me. "I had my hand on the flat iron. I was thinking — *it's too soon. They don't know me well enough. I haven't earned it yet.*"

(Earned it. Let that sit for a second.)

She walked out the door with her hair exactly how it grew out of her head.

"The elevator was dead quiet," she said. "I could hear my own heels. Some man in a gray suit looked at me and looked away. I stared straight ahead."

She got to her desk. Put her bag down. Opened her laptop.

By lunch — three people had complimented her.

But that's not the part that made her call me at 9:47 PM.

By 3 PM — a partner stopped her in the hallway. Not about a case. Not about a brief.

About her HAIR.

A White woman. In a blazer that probably cost more than Tamara's first month's rent. Standing in the hallway of a firm Tamara had been hiding in for three weeks — asking HER about HER hair routine.

"Michelle," she said — and she did that laugh. The kind where you're also sort of crying?

"I spent three weeks hiding. And today I just... showed up as myself."

She paused.

"And the room adjusted."

---

I hung up the phone.

Sat there for a second.

Looked over at the bag sitting on my desk — the one I pack for every meeting where I know someone's going to underestimate me.

Because I've BEEN Tamara, Sis.

I've been the woman at the counter. Hand on the flat iron. Thinking *it's too soon. They don't know me well enough.*

And I got tired of it.

That's why I built these bags. Not so you could carry your laptop. Not so you could look the part for people who've already decided you don't.

So that when you walk into a room that wasn't built for you — you walk in carrying something that already knows who you are.

Even when the room hasn't figured it out yet.
