I was standing in the kitchen at 6 AM on Tuesday.

Coffee in my hand. Phone in the other.

And I read something that made me set the mug down.

A law student at Florida A&M — an HBCU, a Historically Black university — made a flyer for a Black History Month event.

And someone on staff told her to take the word “Black” off the flyer.

At a Black university.

During Black History Month.

My jaw went tight. Not angry. Past angry. That quiet where your voice drops to a place only the people who love you recognize.

George came downstairs.

“What happened?”

I held up my phone. Let him read it himself.

He looked at me. Looked back at the phone. Looked at me again.

“That’s insane,” he said.

“Yeah.” I picked my coffee back up. “That’s February in America.”

He went back upstairs.

 

I just stood there. Thinking about instincts.

The instinct that says: Maybe tone it down. Maybe make it… less.

Less Black.
Less loud.
Less visible.
Less YOU.

You know that instinct?

Because I KNOW you do.

I know you do because two weeks ago, a woman wrote to me. She works at a firm downtown. Big table. Glass walls. The kind of conference room where everything echoes.

She’d done her braids the night before. Took four hours. Gold beads at the ends. She felt GOOD walking in Monday morning.

And her manager looked at her — in front of twelve people — and said: “That’s a lot.”

Not mean. Not yelling. Just… that tone. The one that sounds like a suggestion but lands like a verdict.

She told me she almost took them out that night. Almost.

And then she looked at the bag on her desk.

I Am Enough.

She left the braids in.

 

Women have been doing that — choosing not to disappear — for a long time.

Ninety years ago today — February 21, 1936 — a baby girl was born in Houston, Texas.

Her name was Barbara Jordan.

She grew up to become the first Black woman elected to the Texas State Senate. And in 1976, she walked to a podium at the Democratic National Convention — the FIRST Black woman to deliver the keynote — and said words so heavy that an entire convention hall went quiet:

“What the people want is very simple — they want an America as good as its promise.”

She didn’t whisper that. She DECLARED it. Into a microphone. To a room full of people who didn’t always want to hear it.

Barbara Jordan did not tone it down.

 

Neither do I.

When I design bags, I don’t think about what’s safe to put on them. I think about what needs to be SAID.

The WORDS.

Black Mom Magic.
Phenomenal Woman.
I Am Enough.

I put those words ON the bag. On the outside. Because some things need to be said OUT LOUD.

Not whispered. Not hidden. Not deleted from the flyer.

And somewhere this morning, a woman is reaching for her Afroyla bag. She’s walking out the door. And the words on her shoulder are doing what that law student’s poster was supposed to do.

Declaring.

Not asking.

 

If you’ve ever been told you’re too much —

You’re not. You’re enough. And your bag should say so.

Say it loud →

With all my loud, proud, un-deletable love,
Michelle
