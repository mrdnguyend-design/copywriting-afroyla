# DAILY | 240326 | She Organized The March

**Subject Line:** Guess what today is.
**Preview Text:** (It's not about cheesesteaks.)

---

{{ person.first_name|default:'Love' }},

Guess what today is.

Go ahead. Google it.

National Cheesesteak Day.

I know. I KNOW. Very important day. George just walked past my office and said, "Michelle. It's Cheesesteak Day. Aren't you going to send an email about it?"

George. I'm not sending an email about cheesesteaks.

"Why not? Everybody loves cheesesteaks."

GEORGE.

(He's still standing in the doorway. Chewing. I'll let you guess what he's chewing.)

Anyway.

March 24th IS a special day. Just not because of cheesesteaks.

---

It's the day my favorite actress made history.

March 24, 2002.

Halle Berry walked onto that Oscar stage and became the FIRST Black woman to win Best Actress. Ever. In 74 years of the Academy Awards.

She didn't have a speech prepared. She didn't think she'd win.

So when they called her name, she just... cried.

And then she said something I've never forgotten:

*"This moment is so much bigger than me. This is for Dorothy Dandridge, Lena Horne, Diahann Carroll. It's for the women that stand beside me — Jada Pinkett, Angela Bassett, Vivica Fox. And it's for every nameless, faceless woman of color that now has a chance because this door tonight has been opened."*

That was 24 years ago.

She's still the only one.

Let that sit for a second.

---

But here's the part that wrecked me.

Halle talked about a door being opened.

I wanted to know: who BUILT that door?

So I looked up what else happened on March 24th.

And I found her.

**Dorothy Height.**

Born March 24, 1912.

You probably haven't heard of her. Most people haven't. And THAT is the point.

Dorothy Height spent 80 years fighting for civil rights. She was president of the National Council of Negro Women for FORTY YEARS. She fought for voting rights. She fought against poverty. She organized communities when organizing could get you killed.

And in 1963, she helped organize the March on Washington.

THE March on Washington.

She worked the logistics. She raised the funds. She convinced the organizers to let a young preacher from Atlanta get the microphone.

His name was Martin Luther King Jr.

She made that moment possible.

And when it was time for HER to speak?

They said no.

"You're a woman."

So Dorothy Height stood on that stage. Watched the man SHE helped put there deliver a speech that changed the world.

And she didn't say a word.

---

She didn't stop, though.

She kept going. For forty MORE years. No spotlight. No Oscar. No speech that went viral.

Just the work.

Presidential Medal of Freedom in 1994. Congressional Gold Medal in 2004.

She was 92 years old before the country officially said, "We see you."

---

I sat at my desk after reading all this. Coffee cold. Screen blurring.

And I thought about what she taught me without ever meeting me.

She taught me: you don't wait for someone to hand you a microphone.

You don't wait for the room to be ready.

You don't wait for the door to be opened, or for someone to say your name.

You show up. You do the work. You stand where you're supposed to stand.

And if nobody gives you a crown?

You give yourself one.
