---

March 24, 2002.

I was 16. Pajamas on. Half-asleep on my grandmother's couch. The Oscars were on and honestly I was more interested in the popcorn bowl than the categories.

Then they called Halle Berry's name.

And this woman — MY woman — walked up to that stage and just... broke. Shaking. Couldn't breathe. Couldn't talk. The whole audience is watching her cry and nobody stopped her because everybody in that room knew what this meant.

Then she said:

*"This moment is so much bigger than me. It's for every nameless, faceless woman of color that now has a chance because this door tonight has been opened."*

My grandmother grabbed my hand. Didn't say a word. Just squeezed it.

I remember thinking: *Why is everybody crying? She WON. Isn't this supposed to be happy?*

Girl. I was 16.

Now I'm grown. And I gotta tell you — that speech hits DIFFERENT at 40.

Because it's been 24 years. And Halle Berry is STILL the only Black woman to win Best Actress.

Twenty. Four. Years.

Not a single other one.

(I'll wait while you process that. Take your time. I needed five minutes this morning.)

---

OK so here's where this Tuesday took a TURN.

I'm sitting here with my cold coffee, looking up what else happened on March 24th. You know, just being a nerd at 7 AM. Normal behavior.

And I come across **Dorothy Height**.

Born TODAY. 1912. Richmond, Virginia.

And listen — I need you to clear your schedule for the next two minutes because this woman's story is INSANE.

Dorothy Height gave 80 years of her life to civil rights. She ran the National Council of Negro Women for FORTY years. That's not a career. That's a whole LIFESTYLE.

She fought for voting rights. She organized communities. She was in the room with Dr. King, with A. Philip Randolph, with all of them.

And in 1963? She helped put together the March on Washington.

And I don't mean "helped" like she brought the napkins.

She did the logistics. She raised the money. She's the reason a young preacher from Atlanta got behind that podium.

That preacher? Martin Luther King Jr.

Dorothy Height put. Him. There.

Now here's the part where I had to get up and walk around my kitchen because I was TOO HEATED to keep reading:

When it was HER turn to speak?

"You're a woman."

That's what they told her.

MA'AM.

This woman BUILT the stage. She built the CROWD. She built the MOMENT. And they told her to sit down because she was wearing the wrong body.

(George walked in during this part and said "What's wrong?" I said "EVERYTHING IS WRONG, GEORGE." He backed all the way out. Smart man. 😂)

So Dorothy Height stood there. Right there on that stage. And she watched the man she championed deliver the most famous speech in American history.

And then she went back to work. Quietly. For another 40 years.

She was 92 years old before this country gave her the Congressional Gold Medal and finally said, "We see you, Dorothy."

---

You know what's funny? Not funny ha-ha. Funny like... ohhh.

Halle Berry stood on a stage in 2002 and talked about a door being opened.

But Dorothy Height? She's the one who built the door, hung it, and held it open for everyone else.

And nobody even knew her name.

That's the woman I want to celebrate today. Not the one who walked through the door. The one who BUILT it.

And I bet you know a woman like that.

The one who trained the new guy who got the promotion. The one who organized Thanksgiving and Christmas and EVERY reunion and nobody said thank you. The one who holds it ALL together and nobody thinks to ask if she's okay.
