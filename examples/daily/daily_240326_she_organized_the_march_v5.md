---

March 24, 2002.

I was 16. Sitting on my grandmother's couch in my pajamas. The Oscars were on. I was more interested in the popcorn bowl than the categories.

Then they called Halle Berry's name.

And this woman walked up to that stage and just... stopped. She opened her mouth. Nothing came out. The tears came first. Then: *"Oh my God. Oh my God. I'm sorry."*

The whole room let her cry. Nobody rushed her. Nobody played the music. They just waited.

Then she said:

*"This moment is so much bigger than me. This is for Dorothy Dandridge, Lena Horne, Diahann Carroll. It's for the women that stand beside me, Jada Pinkett, Angela Bassett, Vivica Fox. And it's for every nameless, faceless woman of color that now has a chance because this door tonight has been opened."*

My grandmother grabbed my hand. Didn't look at me. Just squeezed.

I was 16. I thought, *She won. Why is she crying? Isn't this the happy part?*

Girl. I was so young.

Now I'm at this desk, 40 years old, and that speech plays in my head like it was yesterday. Because that was 24 years ago. And Halle Berry is STILL the only Black woman to win Best Actress.

The only one. In 24 years. After that door was supposedly "opened."

(I'll wait while you sit with that.)

---

But you know what else happened on March 24th?

This is where my whole morning went sideways.

**Dorothy Height** was born on this day. 1912.

And I need you to buckle up for two minutes because when I tell you this woman's story? You're going to need to text somebody.

Dorothy Height gave 80 years of her life to civil rights. Not a fundraiser here and a speech there. EIGHTY YEARS. She ran the National Council of Negro Women for 40 of those years. That's not a career. That's a whole entire LIFE.

And in 1963 — pay attention now — she helped organize the March on Washington.

I don't mean she "helped" like she handed out flyers. She ran the LOGISTICS. She raised the MONEY. She's the one who said, "There's this preacher in Atlanta. Put him on the stage."

That preacher? Martin Luther King Jr.

Dorothy Height looked at a 34-year-old pastor and said, *"That's the one."*

She was RIGHT.

So here's the part that nearly made me throw my coffee.

After she PUT him on that stage. After she built the whole operation. When it was her turn to speak?

"You're a woman."

That's what they told her.

(I had to get up from my desk. George walked in and said, "What happened?" I said "NOTHING. EVERYTHING. I CAN'T TALK RIGHT NOW, GEORGE." He backed all the way out. Smart man. 😂)

She stood on that stage. She watched the man SHE believed in deliver the most famous speech in American history. 250,000 people.

And Dorothy Height? She went back to work. For another 40 years.

She was 92 years old when this country finally gave her the Congressional Gold Medal and said, "We see you."

Ninety-two. Y'all.

---

I want to tell you what I felt this morning reading all this.

I didn't feel inspired.

I felt SEEN.

Because I know this woman. Not Dorothy Height personally. I know her STORY. Because it's everywhere.

It's the woman at your job who trained three managers and they all got promoted over her. She's still there. Still training the next one.

It's your mama who worked doubles so you could go to school and act like stress wasn't eating her alive. She said she was "fine." Every single time.

It's your auntie who held the ENTIRE family together through every funeral, every crisis, every Thanksgiving, every Christmas. And nobody thought to ask, "Sis, are YOU okay?"

We ALL know a Dorothy.

We might BE a Dorothy.

She's not going to buy herself a bag. She never does. She'll say, "I don't need anything."
