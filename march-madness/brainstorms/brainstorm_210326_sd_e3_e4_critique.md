"Day 2 is live. But before I show you the new products, I need to be honest with you about something." Good tension. The "but" creates a stop. However...

"I can't describe how grateful I am" — TELL, not SHOW. Michelle would say something like "I stared at the order numbers until my eyes blurred." The email reads more like a brand statement than Michelle. The apology email was more personal. This one reverts to marketing voice.

**Status Positioning:** ⭐⭐⭐⭐ / 5
"Something I was saving for our Mother's Day campaign" — excellent insider reveal. Makes the Mini Satchels feel earned, not reactive.

**Sales Psychology:** ⭐⭐⭐ / 5
The product list with basketball emojis (🏀) feels like a newsletter section, not Michelle writing to you. The apology email had ZERO formatting tricks. This should match.

**Line-by-line improvements:**
- Line: "I can't describe how grateful I am for the way you showed up."
  → Cut. Too corporate. The numbers speak for themselves.
  → Why: "I'm grateful" = brand talk. Show the gratitude through the Mother's Day reveal instead.

- Line: "I stayed up late sorting through it. It was not fun."
  → Not present anymore (good — was cut from earlier version) BUT... the current version has NO personal detail about how Michelle experienced the issue. The apology email has "When I saw this last night." SD-E3 needs something similar.

- Line: "I pulled them off the regular collection shelf because I wanted to make Day 2 feel like it was worth coming back for."
  → Too marketing. "Worth coming back for" = brand positioning language. Simpler: "I pulled them off the regular collection shelf."
  → Why: The facts speak. Don't narrate the strategy.

- Line: "Even if yesterday went perfectly, these bags would've made today special. The fact that yesterday DIDN'T go perfectly? Now they feel necessary."
  → NOT present in current draft (was V1). Good.

### 🎨 DANIEL'S REVIEW

**Scene Construction:** ⭐⭐ / 5
There IS no scene. The apology email didn't need one — it was transactional. But THIS email is announcing new products. It needs at least ONE moment. Not a 4AM desk scene. Something like: the moment she decided to pull the Mother's Day stock. One line. That's enough.

**Dialogue:** ⭐ / 5
Zero dialogue. No Alice, no George. For a sale email to the most engaged segment, this feels flat. Even one line from Alice would add life: Alice saw the Mini Satchels going up on the site and said: "You're really doing this." Yes. I'm really doing this.

**Show vs Tell:** ⭐⭐ / 5
"I can't describe how grateful I am" = telling. "220+ orders" = showing. The email has both, but leans too much on telling. Cut the tells, let the numbers and the Mother's Day reveal do the showing.

**Emotional Pacing:** ⭐⭐⭐ / 5
The arc (honest → pulled bags → Mother's Day reveal → product list → CTA) is structurally sound, but the second half deflates. Once we get past the reveal, it becomes a product listing. The apology email never felt like a listing. This one does at the end.

**Line-by-line improvements:**
- Line: The entire product list (🏀 Wallets from $9.95, etc.)
  → Problem: This breaks the personal letter feeling. The apology email had NO bullet list. This is supposed to match that voice.
  → Better: Weave the products into narrative. "Mini Wallets and Makeup Bags at $9.95. Brand new today. And the Mini Satchels — $29.95, normally $109.95. Straight off the regular shelf."
