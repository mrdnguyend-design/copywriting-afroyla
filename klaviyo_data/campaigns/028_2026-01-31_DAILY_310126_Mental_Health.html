Last week I was invited to a career guidance session for a friend's daughter.

She's a cute, but slightly shy 17-year-old girl.

The talk was about future career paths and how to build a roadmap—standard stuff, right?

Everything was normal until her mother went to the restroom.

That's when this girl leaned in and asked me, in almost a whisper:

"How do you build confidence when you're not getting validation from others? Like... how do you love yourself when it feels like nobody sees you?"

I sat back.

Because WOW.

Seventeen years old and already asking the question that most grown women are still wrestling with at 35.

(I know I was.)

So I told her the truth.

I said:

"Girl, that level of self-awareness? Shoot, you already doing self-love on hard mode. You're already in the game.

For me, external validation is basically emotional fast food. Hits fast, feels good in the moment, but leaves you bloated and wondering why you ever trusted strangers on the internet to tell you you're cute.

Real self-love? That's not a ~vibe~. It's a daily decision. Like drinking water or not texting that emotionally unavailable man just because Mercury's in retrograde."

(And listen—I STILL catch myself doing it sometimes. Last week I posted a photo and checked my phone 47 times in an hour. We're all works in progress.)

She laughed. (Her mom came back right after that line. Timing! 😅)

But I kept thinking about that question all week.

Because here's what nobody talks about:

1 in 5 Black women are struggling with their mental health right now.

Twenty percent.

And that number is rising.

Depression. Anxiety. Burnout. Grief that sits in your chest like a stone you can't name.

We're out here being "strong" for everyone else—holding down jobs, raising kids, showing up for friends, building businesses—while quietly falling apart in the shower at 11 PM.

And you know what makes me FURIOUS?

The fact that we're still being told to "just meditate" or "practice gratitude" like that's gonna fix systemic racism, medical gaslighting, and generational trauma.

Becky at yoga class thinks downward dog is the answer to everything. Meanwhile, we're out here carrying weight Becky will never have to lift.

So no—therapy isn't "just for crazy people." And needing help doesn't make you weak. It makes you SMART.

Here's what helped me start coming home to myself:

🔥 Therapy. Weekly. Without apology. It's not a luxury. It's a utility.

🔥 Solo dates. I sit across from myself in public like I'm the guest of honor, because I AM‼️

🔥 Keeping micro-promises. I do my workout when I say I will. I follow through with me. That builds quiet confidence like nothing else.

Now look—therapy is expensive.

Like really expensive.

I'm talking $150-$300 per session expensive. And if you don't have insurance that covers it? Or your insurance only covers 6 sessions a year? (Which, lol, what kind of math is that?)

You're basically out here choosing between healing and paying rent.

Which is why I want to tell you about The Loveland Foundation.

I've been following their work for a while now, and here's what they do:

They provide free therapy sessions specifically for Black women and girls.

No judgment. No barriers. No "prove you're struggling enough to deserve help."

If you or someone you love needs support but can't afford it, you can apply through them for therapy sessions. Real therapists. Real care. Real support.

👉 Learn more about The Loveland Foundation here

Because here's the truth: We shouldn't have to choose between our mental health and our bills.

Love,
Michelle

P.S. That girl? She texted me two days later and said she booked her first therapy session. Started with the school counselor. Baby steps. But she's in the game now. 💛
