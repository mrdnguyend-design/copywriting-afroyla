I was sitting at the kitchen table last week, planning this whole thing, when I looked at the spot where mama used to line up her pills.

And it all came back.

I was seven.

My mama sat at the kitchen table every Sunday morning with a little brown bottle. She'd pop the cap. Line them up on a napkin. Count.

One. Two. Three. Four. Five.

I watched from the hallway in my pajamas, bare feet on cold tile.

I thought they were candy.

One Sunday, I walked up to that table and reached for one.

My daddy's fork stopped mid-air. My auntie inhaled like I'd said a curse word in church.

Mama looked at me. Looked at the pill in my hand. Took it back — gently — kissed my forehead, and whispered:

"These aren't candy, baby."

Nobody moved. Nobody explained.

I put my hand down, walked back to the hallway, and sat on that cold tile until my feet went numb.

I didn't ask again.

And I wasn't the only one.

That was the rule in our house. Don't ask about the bottle. Don't ask why auntie showed up to Thanksgiving with sugar-free pound cake. Don't ask why mama's little brown bottle got bigger. Why the count went from five pills to eight.

Type 2 diabetes sat at our kitchen table my entire life. And we treated it like a guest nobody invited but everybody pretended wasn't there.

I was forty years old before I said those words out loud to anyone outside my family.

Because that's what silence does. It doesn't protect you. It just makes the thing easier to pretend isn't there.

Then last year, my cousin Dee called me. We grew up at that same kitchen table. She'd been quietly fighting her own version of mama's battle for years — prediabetic since her late twenties. But six months before this call, she'd joined a program: CYL2, "Change Your Lifestyle. Change Your Life." — run by the Black Women's Health Imperative.

She said five words I'll never forget:

"My A1C came back normal."

That's the blood sugar test — the one that tells you whether the diabetes is winning. First time in eleven years it came back clean.

I sat down on the edge of the bed and just held the phone. Didn't say anything. Just listened to her breathe.

Dee didn't get that from a pamphlet. She got it from twelve months of real coaching — the only diabetes prevention program in this country designed by Black women, for Black women. The women who finish it cut their risk by 58%.

My mama didn't have that. My auntie didn't have it. Dee does. And it changed her life.
