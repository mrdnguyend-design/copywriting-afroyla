## 💡 IDEAS

### IDEA 1: "Michelle's Madness" — The Spreadsheet Scene

- **Source:** Brief Story 3 + Accountant joke
- **Hot Dog:** Michelle sitting in front of a pricing spreadsheet. $49.95 wallet. She types $9.95. Stares at it. Deletes it. Types it again. "Madness," she whispers. Hits Enter. George walks in: "What are you doing?" "Something stupid." "...Can I help?"
- **Broccoli:** March Madness sale announcement — $9.95, Mar 20-22, text AFR995E
- **Law:** #1 (Personality — Michelle + George) + #15 (Intimate Details — spreadsheet, cursor blinking)
- **Core Value:** Generosity, founder vulnerability
- **Subject Lines:**
  - "She stared at the price for 15 minutes."
  - "Michelle did something to the spreadsheet."
  - "$9.95. She typed it. Deleted it. Typed it again."
- **Potential:** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ — Founder vulnerability = trust. George comedic beat. Sets up WHY these prices.

### IDEA 2: "A Raisin in the Sun" — Dream Deferred × March Madness

- **Source:** March 11 = Hansberry premiere (1959) + "Treat Yourself" review
- **Hot Dog:** March 11, 1959. A 29-year-old Black woman watched her play open on Broadway. Critics said it wouldn't work. Audiences said otherwise. It ran for 530 performances. Langston Hughes asked "What happens to a dream deferred?" Lorraine Hansberry answered: "You put it on stage anyway." → Bridge: that KD review — "I promised myself one day I would treat myself, but I couldn't choose... then one day she found me." Some dreams get deferred. Some get answered at $9.95.
- **Broccoli:** March Madness sale — your "one day" is March 20
- **Law:** #10 (Historical Analogies) + Customer review
- **Core Value:** Dreams, persistence, self-worth
- **Subject Lines:**
  - "What happens to a dream deferred?"
  - "She was 29 years old. They said it wouldn't work."
  - "March 11, 1959. A Black woman did something impossible."
- **Potential:** ⭐⭐⭐⭐ — Beautiful historical hook. But might be too literary for a SALE teaser. Works better for daily email than high-urgency sale announcement.

### IDEA 4: "The Niece Who Tried to Borrow It" — Small Goods Story
