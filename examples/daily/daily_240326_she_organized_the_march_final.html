March 24, 2002.

I was 16. Sitting on my grandmother's couch in my pajamas, more interested in the popcorn bowl than the categories. The Oscars were on. I wasn't really watching.

Then they called Halle Berry's name.

She walked up to that stage. Opened her mouth. Nothing came out. The tears came first. Then: "Oh my God. Oh my God. I'm sorry."

The whole room let her cry. Nobody rushed her. Nobody played the music. They just waited.

Then she said:

"This moment is so much bigger than me. This is for Dorothy Dandridge, Lena Horne, Diahann Carroll. It's for the women that stand beside me, Jada Pinkett, Angela Bassett, Vivica Fox. And it's for every nameless, faceless woman of color that now has a chance because this door tonight has been opened."

My grandmother grabbed my hand. Didn't look at me. Just squeezed.

I remember thinking, She won. Why is she crying? Isn't this the happy part?

Girl. I was so young.

That was 24 years ago. Halle Berry is STILL the only Black woman to win Best Actress. The only one. After that door was supposedly "opened."

---

But you know what else happened on March 24th?

This is where my whole morning went sideways.

Dorothy Height was born on this day. 1912.

And I need you to buckle up for two minutes because when I tell you this woman's story, you're going to want to text somebody.

Dorothy Height gave 80 years of her life to civil rights. Not a fundraiser here and a speech there. EIGHTY. YEARS. She ran the National Council of Negro Women for 40 of those. That's not a job. That's a whole entire life.

And in 1963, she helped organize the March on Washington.

I don't mean she "helped" like she handed out flyers. She ran the logistics. She raised the money. She convinced the organizers to let a young preacher from Atlanta take the microphone.

That preacher? Martin Luther King Jr.

Dorothy Height championed a 34-year-old pastor nobody outside of Atlanta had heard of. She fought for him to get that slot.

She was right.

So here's the part that nearly made me throw my coffee across the room.

After she built the whole operation. After she put him on that stage. When it was finally HER turn to speak?

"You're a woman."

That's what they told her.

And those men have been on the wrong side of history ever since.

(I had to get up from my desk. George walked in and said, "What happened?" I said, "NOTHING. EVERYTHING. I CAN'T TALK RIGHT NOW, GEORGE." He backed all the way out. Smart man. 😂)

So Dorothy Height stood on that stage. 250,000 people in front of her. She watched the man SHE believed in deliver the most famous speech in American history.

And then she went back to work. Quietly. For another forty years.

She was 92 years old when this country finally handed her the Congressional Gold Medal and said, "We see you, Dorothy."

Ninety-two. Y'all.
