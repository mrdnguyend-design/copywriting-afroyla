March 24th IS a special day. Just not because of cheesesteaks.

---

March 24, 2002.

I was 12. Sitting on my grandmother's couch. The Oscars were on. I was barely paying attention.

Then they called Halle Berry's name.

And this woman walked up to that stage and completely fell apart. Shaking. Sobbing. Couldn't get the words out.

Then she said this:

*"This moment is so much bigger than me. It's for every nameless, faceless woman of color that now has a chance because this door tonight has been opened."*

My mom walked out of the kitchen. Phone still in her hand. Neither of us said anything for a long time.

I was 12. I didn't really get it.

Now I'm grown. And I gotta tell you — I kinda wish I didn't get it.

Because that was 24 years ago. And Halle Berry is STILL the only Black woman to win Best Actress.

Still.

The only one.

---

But that's not even the part that messed me up this morning.

This morning I looked up what ELSE happened on March 24th.

And I found **Dorothy Height**.

Born today. 1912.

(I'm about to ruin your whole Tuesday. You're welcome.)

This woman gave 80 years of her life to civil rights. She ran the National Council of Negro Women for FORTY years. Organized communities when organizing could get you killed. Raised funds, built coalitions, fought for voting rights.

And in 1963? She helped organize the March on Washington.

Not "helped" like she passed out flyers. She did the LOGISTICS. She raised the MONEY. She's the one who convinced the organizers to let a young preacher from Atlanta take the mic.

That preacher's name? Martin Luther King Jr.

Dorothy Height put him on that stage.

And when it was her turn to speak?

They told her no.

*"You're a woman."*

(I had to put my phone down when I read that. I walked around my kitchen for five minutes. George asked me what was wrong. I said "EVERYTHING, George.")

So Dorothy Height stood on that stage. Watched the man she put there deliver the most famous speech in American history.

And she went back to work.

For FORTY more years. No spotlight. No Oscar. No viral moment.

She was 92 years old before this country gave her the Presidential Medal of Freedom and said, "We see you."

Ninety-two.

---

You know what I keep thinking?

Halle Berry talked about a door being opened.

But Dorothy Height BUILT that door. She poured the concrete. She hung the hinges. She held it open.

And nobody even knew her name.

And somehow that feels... familiar.

Not just to me. To every woman reading this.

The one who trained the man who got promoted over her. The one who organized the family reunion nobody thanked her for. The one who holds it together every day and nobody asks if SHE'S okay.
