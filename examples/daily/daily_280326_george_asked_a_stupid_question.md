# DAILY | 280326 | George Asked A Stupid Question

**Subject Line:** "She got her daddy's nose."
**Preview Text:** Six words at a dinner table. Twenty-five years of silence.

---

{{ person.first_name|default:'love' }},

George, my husband, was in the office yesterday.

(This is already a problem. George in my office is like a toddler in a pottery shop. You just wait for the crash.)

He walked around. Looked at the bags on display. Touched things he shouldn't be touching. Then he stopped in front of one of the Affirmation pieces on the wall.

Squinted at it. Tilted his head.

"Why do the bags need WORDS on them? They're bags."

I looked at him. Looked at the bag. Didn't answer.

Because the real answer isn't about George. It's about my friend Toni.

---

Toni came by the office years ago. Before any of this was real. Sketches taped to the walls, coffee rings on every surface. The Affirmation Collection was just drawings and sticky notes back then.

She walked around. Looked at the sketches. Stopped at one near the window.

Didn't touch it. Just stood there, reading the word on it. Her lips moved a little like she was saying it to herself.

I figured she was being polite. You know how your friends do when they visit your little "project." They smile. They nod. They say *"oh that's dope"* and then y'all go get lunch.

We went and got lunch.

She hugged me around 5. Said she loved what I was doing. Normal Toni.

Then later that night my phone buzzed.

A text:

*"Make one that says what I needed to hear when I was 14."*

I called her. She picked up but didn't say anything.

"Toni. What happened when you were 14?"

She laughed. Not a real laugh. The kind you do right before you say something you've never said out loud.

---

When Toni was 14, somebody at the dinner table said it in front of everyone.

*"She got her daddy's nose."*

Six words. The table laughed. Somebody passed the mac and cheese. Dinner kept going.

Toni stopped chewing. Looked down at her plate. Didn't pick her fork back up.

It wasn't even mean. That's the thing. It was apologetic. Like *"poor thing, she got the wrong one."*

At 14, she started holding her nose up in pictures so it looked more pointed.

At 16, she was pressing the bridge every night thinking she could reshape it.

At 22, she'd trained herself to turn slightly left whenever someone pulled out a camera. Automatic. Like blinking.

At 30, she was still doing it.

(She told me she was scared of getting PREGNANT because somebody said your nose gets bigger. Girl. PREGNANT.)

Twenty-five years. That's how long six words at a dinner table lasted.

---

Then one afternoon she walked into my messy little office. Looked at those sketches. And for the first time I realized she hadn't been polite that day. She'd been QUIET. The way you get quiet when something hits you and you don't have words for it yet.

She went home. Found the words at 11 o'clock at night.

*Make one that says what I needed to hear when I was 14.*

Because at that dinner table, somebody should have looked right at her and said **"And it's BEAUTIFUL."**

Nobody did.

I pulled up the Blacknificent sketch after that text. Stared at it for a while. Thought about how many women are walking around carrying a sentence somebody said to them at 14. At 11. At 9. A sentence nobody ever went back and corrected.

That's why I made these bags, George.

---

So to answer my husband's question:

*"Why do the bags need words on them?"*

Because some women went 25 years without hearing the right ones.
