---

It's the day my favorite actress made history.

March 24, 2002.

I was 12.

Sitting on my grandmother's couch. My mom was on the phone in the kitchen. The Oscars were on and I wasn't really watching. I was half-asleep. Bored.

Then they called Halle Berry's name.

And this woman — this beautiful, Black woman — walked up to that stage and FELL APART.

She was shaking. Sobbing. She couldn't even get the words out for a good 30 seconds.

And then she said this:

*"This moment is so much bigger than me. This is for Dorothy Dandridge, Lena Horne, Diahann Carroll. And it's for every nameless, faceless woman of color that now has a chance because this door tonight has been opened."*

My mom walked into the living room. Phone still in her hand. Neither of us said anything.

I didn't understand it then.

I understand it now.

That was 24 years ago. Halle Berry is STILL the only Black woman to win Best Actress. Twenty-four years. And nobody else.

(Are you kidding me?)

So this morning, I'm thinking about Halle's speech. The door she talked about. And I wanted to know — who built that door?

I Googled it.

And I found a woman I should've known about my entire life.

---

**Dorothy Height.**

Born TODAY. March 24, 1912.

And I need you to hear me on this because I'm still not over it.

This woman gave EIGHTY YEARS of her life to civil rights. Not a typo. EIGHTY.

She ran the National Council of Negro Women for 40 years. That's longer than most marriages. Longer than most careers. Longer than most people's PATIENCE.

She fought for voting rights when fighting could get you lynched. She organized communities when organizing could get you disappeared.

And in 1963, she helped put together the March on Washington.

THE March on Washington.

Picture this. August 28, 1963. A quarter million people on the National Mall. The Washington Monument behind them. A young preacher from Atlanta is about to give the speech of his life.

Dorothy Height is standing on that stage. She coordinated the logistics. She raised the funds. She's the one who convinced the organizers to give that young preacher the microphone.

His name was Martin Luther King Jr.

She. Made. That. Moment. Possible.

And when it was time for HER to speak?

"You're a woman."

That's what they said.

(I need you to read that sentence again. Because I read it this morning and I had to put my phone DOWN and walk around my kitchen for five minutes before I could keep reading.)

So Dorothy Height stood on that stage. Right there. Watched the man SHE put behind that podium change history.

And she didn't say a word.

She didn't scream. She didn't cry. She didn't walk off.

She just stood there. And then she went back to work.

For forty MORE years.

No Oscar. No speech that went viral. No Wikipedia page until she was almost 90.

Presidential Medal of Freedom in 1994. Congressional Gold Medal in 2004.

She was 92 years old before this country officially said, "We see you."

(NINETY-TWO.)

---

I want to tell you what I felt when I read this.

I felt ANGRY.

Not inspired.

Angry.

Because I know what it's like to build something and have someone else get the credit. I know what it's like to be told "it's not your turn yet." I know what it's like to walk into a room that wasn't designed for me and have to act like that's fine.

And I know — I KNOW — every woman on this list knows that feeling too.

The feeling of being overlooked. Passed over. Talked across.

The feeling of being asked to do the hard work and then being asked to sit down while someone else takes the mic.

So here's what Dorothy Height taught me this morning.

She didn't fight for 80 years because somebody gave her permission.

She didn't organize the March on Washington because somebody asked her to.

She did it because she couldn't NOT do it.

And when they told her to sit down?

She sat down. And then she built 40 more years of change from that chair.

That's not patience. That's POWER.
