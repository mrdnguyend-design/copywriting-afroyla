6:47 this morning. Kitchen. Nia walked in holding her phone like evidence.

"Mom. Do you know who Victor Glover is?"

The astronaut, baby. Artemis II. Yes, I know.

"OK but I found something really interesting." She sat down. "Did you know his grandfather was in the Air Force too?"

I didn't know that.

"He wanted to fly. Just like his grandson."

OK...

"But they told him no."

Why?

She looked at me. "Because he was Black."

She let that sit for a second. Then:

"But Mom. His grandson just flew to the MOON."

I didn't say anything.

"Like... they told his grandfather he couldn't even sit in a cockpit. And his grandson went to SPACE. How is that not the coolest thing you've ever heard?"

It is, baby.

She scrolled her phone. "And you know what he said?" She read it out loud, slow:

"I live in the America that sent me to space, and told my grandfather he couldn't fly during the Korean conflict when he was enlisted, but he got to sit and watch me fly."

She looked at me like: are you getting this?

I was getting it. I just couldn't talk yet.

Here's what stayed with me all morning. Nia told that story like the ending was obvious. Like OF COURSE a Black man ends up orbiting the Moon. What other ending would there be?

I didn't grow up thinking like that. I remember seeing a Black pilot on the news and my first thought was, is that allowed?

Nia would never ask that question. She'd ask which seat.

That might be the most beautiful thing our community ever built. Not a rocket. A generation of children who don't ask permission.

That's why I named a collection Resilient. Because that word isn't about surviving. It's about the carrying. The quiet, stubborn, beautiful carrying.
