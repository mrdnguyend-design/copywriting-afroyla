# DAILY | 270426 | Some Of Us Picked The Career

**Subject:** Some of you are going to hate this email.
**Preview:** Some of you are going to love it. Let's find out together.

---

Some of you are going to hate this email. Some of you are going to love it.

I don't know yet which one you are. I don't think you do either.

Let's find out together.

---

I was reading something on my phone this morning when George walked in, coffee in his hand, bald head shining like he'd polished it.

"Whatcha reading."

"Article about a woman named Emma Grede."

"Should I know her."

"Probably not, but you should. She's one of the most successful businesswomen in the country right now. Built a few brands you'd recognize if I named them. Has a book out."

"Okay. What'd she say."

"She did a profile in the Wall Street Journal a few weeks back. Told them she's a 'max three-hour mom.'"

George set his coffee down and looked at me. "What does that mean."

"It means exactly what it sounds like, George. It means the maximum amount of time she spends with her children, per week, is three hours. And those three hours are on the weekend. The rest of the time she's working, traveling, doing what successful people do. The kids are with the nanny, the school, the husband, somebody. Not her."

George thought about that for a second. "Three hours a week with your own kids."

"Three. That's the cap she put on it. And when people got mad about it, she went on the Breakfast Club and the Today show and said it again. Louder. Said she's not walking it back. Called herself a 'core memory mom.'"

"What's a core memory."

"It's an idea Pixar made up — that movie Inside Out. The theory is your kid only really keeps about five or six big memories from childhood, and those memories shape who they become. So instead of being there for the small stuff every day, you engineer a few big moments — the fishing trip, the trip to New York, the surprise birthday — and you make THOSE land hard. Quality over quantity, but a very specific kind of quality."

"So she's parenting based on a cartoon."

"George."

"What."

"I'm trying to be FAIR here."

George held his hands up and waited.

---

Here's where I land.

She's not wrong. She's a Black woman who built something that hires hundreds of women, and she picked the work the way men have picked the work for a thousand years (on purpose, no apology). That choice is hers and I respect it.

There's a price though. There's always a price. Whoever is in the room when your child is sick at 2 AM, that's who they remember. Whoever's face lights up when they walk in from school, that's who they're looking for. Sometimes that's the mother. Sometimes it's the grandmother, or the auntie, or the woman the family pays to be there. The kid will know who showed up. Sooner or later, the kid will know.

But the knowing is the math she chose. She knew it when she signed up.

---

Then I thought about who actually opens this email every morning, and honestly, most of you don't have that math.

You work eight hours, then you come home and cook dinner, drive carpool, check on Mama, get to church on Sunday and Bible study on Wednesday. That's not "core memory" parenting. That's "the whole house stays standing up" parenting, and it's a different job entirely.

Both kinds of women are real. Both are doing something hard. Neither one is the other one's enemy.

---

I told George all of that and then I told him I was about to sit down and write today's email about it. He drank his coffee and stared at the wall for a minute.

"So what's the email about."

"That."

"That you just told me?"

"That."

He looked at me. "Where's the bag, Michelle."

I looked up at the calendar. "Mother's Day is May 10. Two weeks from this Sunday. The MD pricing is still running, up to 60% off through the 10th. If you want to put one in her hands by Sunday morning, order by May 1."

"That's the bag."

"That's the bag, George."
