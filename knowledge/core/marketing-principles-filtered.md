### Email Writing — Binary Rules

**ALWAYS:**
- **CTA names specific product benefit** — Never end with a bare link. Every CTA names what clicking gives the reader. "Shop the Heritage Tote — the bag that made Karen stop mid-aisle." Not "Shop now."
- **Grade 5 readability** — Short sentences, simple words, frequent line breaks. Read aloud: if you lose breath mid-sentence, cut it.
- **Diagnose before rewriting** — List exact problems first. Never rewrite by feel. (Embedded in /proofread workflow — worth encoding explicitly.)
- **Deliver opt-in bribe in welcome email** — Any VIP/loyalty promise must be delivered in Email 1. Promising without delivering destroys trust immediately.
- **Disclose email frequency in welcome email** — Tell new subscribers what to expect: how often, what kind of content.
- **Embed directional CTA in infotainment content** — Pure entertainment with no CTA = brand building only, not selling. Every email needs at least one link, even if soft.

**NEVER:**
- **NEVER open with "Do you struggle with X?"** — Problem-question openers = weak copywriting. Afroyla opens with story or event.
- **NEVER display weakness, self-pity, or victim framing** — Vulnerability ≠ victimhood. Michelle can be honest about difficulty; she cannot be defeated by it. Consistent with "the prize" positioning.
- **NEVER stuff email body with bullet-point lists** — Wall of bullets signals no argument development is coming. Readers skip to the end.
- **NEVER tease a subject line without payoff** — Unsatisfied curiosity → resentment → unsubscribes.
- **NEVER use first name in subject lines routinely** — Frequent name use reads as bank or spam, not a person.
- **NEVER use a current-events hook without emotional pivot** — News + jump to pitch = non sequitur. The hook must connect emotionally to the product, not just thematically.
- **NEVER give away free samples as primary selling strategy** — Sales copy must be more compelling than the content itself.
- **NEVER submit AI-written sincere/emotional Phase 3 sections as final copy** — AI-manufactured sincerity is hollow. Flag these sections for human (Michelle/Duc) rewrite before sending.
- **NEVER use indirect dialogue when direct quotation is available** — "'What can I get you?' he said" > "He asked what I wanted." Direct quote = immediacy + proof.
- **NEVER dump all surprising information in one sentence** — Reveal progressively. Zoom from general to specific. Pause after each layer.
- **NEVER hedge with defensive phrases** — "I understand some may disagree" / "just my personal opinion" = destroys authority. Afroyla speaks directly.

### Email Writing — Frameworks

**Story Construction:**
- **Running Gag Pattern** — Exactly 3 scenes with same theme; each scene escalates; humor peaks on scene 3. Use when you have 3 small moments that build on each other thematically.
- **Seamless Transition Method** — Connect 2 scenes with ~7-word sentence. Benchmark: Throssell's "Yet before I can laugh too much..." Transitions >25 words = rewrite. Never use "Speaking of which" or "That reminds me" as transitions.
- **Two Options Framework** — Master decision tree: scene has natural hook → Option 1 (Find the Hook in the Story); no hook → Option 2 (Add Something to the Scene: Fiction > Introspection > Association).
- **"Construct a Story Without Storytelling" Method** — For when Duc or Michelle doesn't feel like a "storyteller": just find 2-3 small moments from the day. The frameworks turn them into story. Never stare at a blank page asking "what story do I tell?"

**Transition & Pivot Techniques:**
- **"Find the Hook in the Story"** — Look for: (a) a theme that intersects the scene + the reader's pain, or (b) a specific word/phrase that links naturally to the product.
- **4-Step Transition Sequence** — Spot theme in scene → Apply to self → Generalize to reader → Lead to product. Use when pivot feels forced or artificial.
- **Wording-Based Pivot** — Reuse an exact phrase from the scene as the bridge into pitch. Cleanest pivot when a phrase carries dual meaning.
- **Association Technique** — "This reminds me of..." pivot. Limit: max 1 in every 5 emails. Overuse feels manufactured.
- **Introspection Technique** — Pivot via 2-3 lines of narrator's current thoughts about the scene (not the product). Natural inner-voice transition.
- **Transition Decision: Insight vs. Bolt-On** — If the story generates a genuine insight → use it as transition. No insight? → Go straight to Bolted-On Pitch.
- **Bolted-On Pitch Technique** — When story doesn't naturally lead to product: 8-15 line pitch with 5 elements: **Callback** (scene image) + **Specificity** (product details) + **Persuasion** (why this matters) + **Desire** (what it gives them) + **CTA**.
- **Humorous Callback** — Reference a specific image from the story in the pitch. Creates satisfying circle + lightens the ask after an emotional story.
- **Word Connection Transition** — Take exact phrase from story; use it as the literal bridge sentence into pitch. Specific and seamless.

**Idea Generation:**
- **Q&A Email ("Feel Like Content But Sell")** — Pull a real Afroyla customer question; answer briefly + link. Feels like value email, functions as soft sell. Source: customer-voices/ folder, DMs, TrustPilot.
- **"No Private Drama" Framework** — Convert criticism/conflict into public email content. When someone criticizes Afroyla or Michelle's approach, that's an email. Don't fight in private; turn it into content.
- **"Small Business Owner" Model** — Anti-writer's block: imagine Michelle standing in a physical store talking to a regular customer. What would she say? Generates natural, unforced voice.
- **Story "Inception" Method** — Start from the message/idea you want to plant, then find a story that causes the reader to arrive at that idea themselves. (Contrast: Backwards Email starts from the story and finds the message.)

**Advanced Techniques (use after mastering basics):**
- **Fun Arrogance Formula** — Handle negative reactions with playful superiority (not anger). Authority + entertainment simultaneously. "I'm sorry my $140 bag is offending your $14 one."
- **Shame-Without-Malice Framework** — Tell someone else's mistake; reader recognizes themselves without being directly called out. Teaches without lecturing.
- **Progressive Reveal Technique** — Zoom in layer by layer, pause after each reveal. AI knows WHAT to reveal but not WHEN or HOW MUCH at each step. Pacing is the craft.
- **Dialogue Colour** — Add action beats every 2-3 dialogue lines; use creative verbs instead of "said." Fixes flat AI dialogue. "She set down the bag." not just "said."
- **Internal Monologue for Suspense** — Add narrator's inner thoughts to build tension between events. AI skips this layer. "I didn't say anything. I just kept looking at it."

**Campaign-Specific:**
- **Campaign Recap/Reminder Block** — 2-4 bullet recap of campaign rules in email body from Day 2 onward. New readers understand; returning readers skip. Include price, deadline, mechanic.
- **"TIME'S UP" Urgency Opener** — Multi-day sales only. Open: "Yesterday's offer is gone. [Congratulate buyers.] Today's is..." Pivot fast. Creates freshness even on Day 4.

### Subject Lines

- **Grafting Pre-Test** — Imagine the subject line coming from a rarely-opened friend. If it still gets opened → the subject line is too generic (it's working on sender name, not itself). Rewrite.
- **Double Whammy** — Curiosity + novelty simultaneously. "How to stop panic attacks with a tennis ball" = curiosity (how?) + novelty (tennis ball?). Aim for both.
- **Infotaining Subject Line System (5 angles)** — Rotate through: Innuendo / Contradiction / Curiosity gap / High contrast / Tabloid hook. Breaks subject line pattern fatigue across a campaign.
- **Tabloid Headline Technique** — One bizarre specific detail makes ordinary into arresting. "One-hour undies" worked for a finance newsletter. Ask: what is the strangest TRUE detail in this story?

---

### Bullet Points (Fascinations)

- **Daniel's Three Core Strategies — in order:** Ideas (concept itself creates desire) → Words (power language if idea is weak alone) → Misdirection (open false possibilities, close correct ones). **Never start with Misdirection.** Never skip straight to Word-level work when the Idea is weak.
- **4-Step Bullet Writing Process** — (1) Market research (what does Afroyla's audience desperately want to know?), (2) Scan product for relevant content, (3) Filter for ideas that create desire, (4) ONLY THEN write. Duc: always check customer-voices/ and TrustPilot reviews before writing bullets.
- **Strip-Down Test** — Remove all power words; read the bare idea. If the idea is weak alone → find a NEW idea. Do NOT add more embellishment to a weak idea.
- **Idea Stacking Method** — Add a 2nd or 3rd genuinely new idea into one bullet (not restatements of the same idea with different words). Each addition = distinct new point of desire.
- **Curiosity Gap Sizing** — Narrower gap = better. Two tests: Does the reader WANT to know? Does the reader think they ALREADY know? Both must be true for the gap to work.
- **Curiosity Gap Placement** — Find the jewel (what the market most wants to know). Hide the jewel. Reveal only the supporting detail.
- **Misdirection Technique** — Open possibilities that are false, close possibilities that are true. Powerful but trust-eroding if overused. Only after Ideas + Gap are solid.
- **NEVER write bullet only because content exists in product** — Bullet exists because the content creates genuine desire. Content being in the product is not a reason; the desire it creates is the reason.
- **NEVER reveal the jewel then curiosity around secondary details** — The curiosity gap surrounds the jewel, not its packaging.

### Campaign Planning

- **Three-Phase Power Email Model:**
  - **Phase 1 — Announcement (first 24h):** "Breaking news" vibe. Informative, not persuasive. Drive to sales page. No hard sell.
  - **Phase 2 — Percolate (middle days):** Story-based, entertaining. 1-3 emails/day. Don't try to close. Let the offer sit with readers. Use auxiliary sales ideas (supporting reasons to buy).
  - **Phase 3 — Deadline (final 12-24h):** 3-8 emails. Leads close to offer. Compelling ideas only. Tone: sincere → impassioned. Frequency escalates per hour as deadline nears. → *See ADAPTED version for Afroyla-specific Phase 3 lead rule.*

- **Compelling vs. Auxiliary Sales Ideas** — Test: "If this were the ONLY reason to buy, would readers buy?" → Yes = Compelling (Phase 3). No alone = Auxiliary (Phase 2). Never use Auxiliary ideas in Phase 3; they dilute urgency.

- **Phased Emotional Messaging** — Phase 3 escalation arc: Urgent reminder → Sincere persuasion → Impassioned pleading (last few hours only). Only write the "impassioned pleading" section when you/Michelle genuinely believes in the offer.

- **Buyer Psychology Model** — 3 types: Hyper-buyers (Phase 1, need minimal push), Considerers (Phase 2, need reasons to justify), Fence-sitters (Phase 3, need urgency to decide). Structure serves all three without burning any.

- **Multi-Day Campaign Arc Framework** — 4-7 days. Each day's offer has a true 24h window that genuinely disappears. Surprise pricing changes (real). Variety across days (different products, different price points). Narrative connects each day to the next.

- **Epic Finale Recap Structure** — Final email: recap each previous day's offer briefly, frame as end of the story, present the largest/best final offer. Reader who missed Day 1 still feels the full arc.

- **KLTR Model** — Know / Like / Trust / Revere. Use as 365-day content compass outside of campaigns.
  - Know: Afroyla exists (subject line opens, shares)
  - Like: Michelle's personality (recurring characters, humor, honesty)
  - Trust: delivered on promises, showed receipts, real stories
  - Revere: moments of genuine cultural impact, community recognition, superfan status
  - *George's silence in the final IWD email hit harder than his words because KLTR had been built for months.*

- **NEVER hard-sell from Phase 1 or early Phase 2** — Uniform intensity burns the list. Pacing = 800m race: burst at Phase 1, hold through Phase 2, sprint Phase 3.

- **Escalation triggers requiring human confirmation:**
  - Urgency/scarcity mechanics: confirm deadline is real before writing. If not real → refuse fake urgency.
  - Phase 3 sincere/emotional copy: produce structure, flag sections for Michelle/Duc to rewrite. Do not ship AI-drafted sincere sections.
  - No real scenes or stories available: request raw material from Michelle. Do not fabricate scenes wholesale.
  - Campaign has no real deadline: propose real alternatives first (quantity limit, price increase, bonus expiry) before building Three-Phase scaffold.
  - Opt-in bribe doesn't connect to paid product: confirm bribe has been reverse-engineered from the offer before writing opt-in page.

### Opt-In Funnel

*(See ADAPTED: Potential Energy Diagnostic for SMS-specific version)*

- **Perfect Opt-In Bribe Framework** — 5 criteria, all required: Tangible (they can use it immediately) + Real perceived value (they'd pay for it) + Solves a burning problem (not a nice-to-have) + Not homework (instant delivery, not a course) + Sets up the sale (attracts buyers, not browsers).

- **Homepage Structure Framework** — Pattern: Argument → CTA → Argument → CTA (3-4 repetitions). Stop after 4 arguments. Do NOT publish sample emails on homepage — showing readers what they get removes the reason to subscribe.

- **Please Confirm Page Rescue** — The confirm page has 100% reach and most brands leave it blank. Add: bribe reminder + threat (what they lose if they don't confirm) + curiosity tease for first email.

- **Check Inbox Page Maximizer** — 100% reach. Choose ONE strategy: (a) pre-frame what's coming, (b) soft pitch, (c) tease next email, (d) surprise bonus, (e) deliver instant content.

- **Welcome Email Checklist** — 10 steps: Greet + Excite + Fun/memorable moment + Michelle backstory + Frequency warning + Unsubscribe reminder + Deliver bribe + Soft pitch + Ask for reply + P.S. tease.

- **Welcome Sequence Builder** — Default: NO welcome sequence. Only build a sequence when Afroyla has proven "greatest hits" content. Live emails beat pre-written sequences because they feel current and specific.

- **NEVER ask subscribers to whitelist email** — Whitelist requests are universally ignored. Ask for a reply instead ("Hit reply and tell me your favorite bag color" = same deliverability goal, much higher engagement).

### Review / QC

- **Six-Point Email Diagnostic** — 6 PASS/FAIL checks (in order):
  1. Pacing/storytelling — does the scene flow and build?
  2. Point of story — is there a clear moral or purpose?
  3. Lead — does it pass the Triforce (R+E+B 2/3)?
  4. Argument clarity — does the logic chain hold?
  5. Insight quality — is the lesson genuine or generic?
  6. Sales desire — does reading this make you want the product?
  
  *Annotate each point. Understand WHY it fails before rewriting.*

- **Six Patterns of AI Email Failure** — Names for quick diagnosis: Pacing failure / Missed moral / Lead fails Triforce / Muddled argument / Weak insight / No sales desire. Use before editing any AI-generated draft.

- **Story Moral Assessment** — 4 story purposes: (a) pure entertainment, (b) emotional connection, (c) leads to insight, (d) illustrates product. AI forces a moral onto every story. If the moral is forced → remove it and use Bolted-On Pitch instead.

- **Three Components of Sales Desire** — Story moral + Insight + Pitch. Email needs at least 1 of 3 to create desire. AI emails often have all 3 in form but none in substance.

- **"Imagine the Busy Mother" Visualization** — Before writing: picture a woman with 3 kids and 20 unread emails. Would she stop for this? If no → the premise needs rethinking, not the prose.

- **Email Lead Quality Checklist** — 4 criteria: Brief + vivid / Happening live (present tense feel) / Builds a character / Creates a smile or laugh. Run after writing the lead; before writing the rest.

- **Four Characteristics of Phase 3 Emails** — 4-point check: (1) Frequency (3-8/day). (2) Lead is close to offer. (3) Compelling idea (not auxiliary). (4) Sincere/urgent tone. Fail any one = rewrite.

- **THREE Hurdles Framework** — Three failure points in order: Hurdle 1: subject line (do they open?) → Hurdle 2: perceived reading effort (do they start reading?) → Hurdle 3: lead quality (do they continue?). Hurdle 2 is most commonly failed — walls of text, dense paragraphs, no white space.

- **Price Anchoring** — Anchor to real past transactions only ("retail $140, you're paying $14.95"). Never use abstract value stacking ("a $10,000 value!") without verifiable evidence. Afroyla's price anchors = retail prices on afroyla.com.

- **Social Proof — Dual Layer** — (1) High-status proof: Michelle's credentials, the 868 TrustPilot reviews, specific notable customers. (2) Reader-like proof: raw customer screenshots, voice memos, DM quotes (typos preserved). Both layers serve different trust functions. Use both in campaign emails.

## SECTION 2: ADAPTED

Rules that conflict with or need calibration for Afroyla's brand. **Use the brand version. Ignore the original rule.**

---

### 1. Sustained Negativity

**Original:** NEVER make readers feel consistently sad, guilty, or uncomfortable — sustained negativity destroys the list relationship.

**Brand conflict:** Afroyla uses villain framing as a core persuasion tool. Policing negativity entirely defangs the brand's edge and flattens the cultural story.

**Afroyla version:**
> Villain framing is allowed and encouraged. Afroyla's named enemies: Euro-centric luxury brands that built empires on Black culture then locked Black women out; fast fashion bags that peel in 3 months; society's habit of making Black women "earn" their shine.
>
> **What's banned:** Prolonged self-pity, hopelessness, or leaving the reader in unresolved darkness. Every villain story must exit through a TURN — Michelle's realization, the reader's recognition of their own power, or the product as the answer.
>
> Rule of thumb: **Negativity toward the villain = OK. Negativity that leaves the reader feeling small, defeated, or ashamed = rewrite.**

---

### 2. Phase 3 "No Story Leads"

**Original:** NEVER use story-based leads or topic-unrelated intros in Phase 3 emails.

**Brand consideration:** Michelle's voice IS story-driven. A cold "ENDS TONIGHT" opener without any character presence feels off-brand and may underperform with Afroyla's warm audience.

**Afroyla version:**
> Phase 3 emails MAY open with a **1-2 line scene or character moment** from Michelle as an emotional anchor. But the scene must pivot to the offer **within the first 3 sentences.**
>
> Permitted: *"I checked the dashboard at 6 AM. 47 units left."* → Immediately into offer.
> Not permitted: A 400-word story that reaches the offer in paragraph 7.
>
> **Final 2-3 hours of deadline:** Drop scenes entirely. Pure Michelle voice, sincere and direct. No scene setup needed — the character is already established. Just speak.

---

### 3. Opt-In Funnel — Email Address Only / Double Opt-In

**Original:** ALWAYS ask for email address only at sign-up. ALWAYS enable double opt-in.

**Brand conflict:** Afroyla runs SMS-first acquisition flows. Keyword opt-ins (text "BLACK" to shortcode) are core to the model and cannot be replaced by email-only forms.

**Afroyla version:**
> **Email sign-up:** Email address only, no extra fields. Double opt-in where platform allows.
>
> **SMS opt-in:** Keyword to shortcode only. No email capture on same screen. Keyword confirmation = SMS equivalent of double opt-in. Never ask for name, email, or additional fields in SMS opt-in flow.
>
> **Cross-channel:** Email subscribers are offered SMS upgrade in welcome sequence. SMS subscribers are offered email list during campaign flows. Treat as separate journeys; never force both simultaneously.

---

### 4. "NEVER write email copy as if texting a friend"

**Original:** NEVER write email copy as if genuinely texting a friend — Throssell's standard is "entertainment column for a newspaper," not casual text.

**Brand conflict:** This applies to EMAIL but directly contradicts Afroyla's SMS standard. Style-rules.md Rules 13-16 explicitly require SMS to sound like Michelle texting a friend.

**Afroyla version:**
> **For EMAIL:** Throssell standard applies. Michelle's email voice = entertainment column written by your smartest, funniest friend. The warmth is real; the craft is deliberate. Not a casual text dump.
>
> **For SMS:** Michelle IS texting a friend. Fragments, single thoughts per line, "lol" — just Michelle's day. The "entertainment column" standard does NOT apply to SMS.
>
> **Never apply email voice standards to SMS, or SMS voice standards to email.**

---

### 5. Scene Stacking — Audience Contexts

**Original:** NEVER apply Scene Stacking to cold audiences, welcome emails, transactional emails, urgency/scarcity emails, re-engagement emails, or short promotional emails.

**Afroyla-specific mapping:**

| Afroyla Context | Scene Stacking? |
|---|---|
| MASS list (warm subscribers) | ✅ YES — primary email tool |
| HIGH-ENGAGED segment | ✅ YES — primary email tool, warmer pivot ok |
| REG segment (campaign-registered) | ⚠️ SHORT SCENES ONLY — Phase 2 emails only |
| Phase 3 deadline emails | ❌ MAX 2 LINES — see ADAPTED rule #2 above |
| Welcome email | ❌ NO — character intro, not scene stacking |
| Transactional (order confirm, shipping) | ❌ NO — stay functional |
| Re-engagement sequence | ❌ NO — short, direct, no scene setup |
| SMS (any segment) | ❌ NEVER — SMS is never scene-stacked |

---

### 6. Infotainment Enemy Targeting

**Original:** ALWAYS define and attack a specific enemy in infotainment content — a wrong idea, false guru, bad habit, or harmful mainstream advice.

**Brand version:** Afroyla has pre-defined enemies. Use these; don't invent new ones per email.

> **Primary enemies:** Euro-centric luxury brands (built empires on Black aesthetics, excluded Black women). Fast fashion bags that peel in 3 months. Society's assumption that Black women must hustle twice as hard to deserve luxury.
>
> **Secondary enemies:** The idea that "representation" means a single token campaign. The assumption that "quality" is defined by whatever European houses say it is. Mainstream luxury's silence when Black women are the culture.
>
> **NOT enemies:** Individual people (racism is named only in customer stories, never in Michelle's editorial voice). Competitors by name. Any group that includes the reader or the reader's community.

---

### 7. Potential Energy Diagnostic — Opt-In Funnel

**Original:** 9-stage funnel. At every stage: Bribe + Threat + Curiosity must be present.

**Adaptation:** Afroyla runs two parallel opt-in journeys (email + SMS) with different mechanics.

**Afroyla version:**
> Apply the Potential Energy Diagnostic separately to each journey:
>
> **Email journey:** Homepage → CTA → Please Confirm page → Check Inbox page → Welcome email. Ensure Bribe/Threat/Curiosity at each transition.
>
> **SMS journey:** Keyword CTA (in email or social) → Reply confirmation → First SMS → VIP campaign access. Bribe = early access / exclusive deal. Threat = limited spots. Curiosity = "we're planning something big for this list."
>
> **Cross-channel upgrade points** (email → SMS or SMS → email) are the highest-leverage potential energy moments. Treat each as a mini opt-in with its own Bribe/Threat/Curiosity.

| Rule / Framework | Reason |
|---|---|
| **"No Sales Page" Direct-Email Selling** | Built for info-product sellers who link to an order form. Afroyla sells physical products that require product pages with imagery. Context mismatch — irrelevant. |
| **"Escalate if target market is outside source scope"** | Afroyla IS the target market these frameworks were built for: personality-driven, entertainment-first, solo-brand list. The B2B/healthcare/pharma escalation trigger does not apply. Skip this meta-rule entirely. |
| **"Confirm Throssell-style fits formal brand" escalation** | Afroyla is explicitly a Throssell/Settle-methodology brand. This trigger exists for users unsure whether entertainment frameworks fit their formal corporate brand. Irrelevant. |
| **"Daily email: do not apply to lists above 100K" (context rule)** | Afroyla has 120K+ subscribers AND sends frequent campaigns. This restriction was written for generic corporate brands whose audiences didn't opt in expecting personality content. Afroyla's audience opted into Michelle's world. Reject the threshold; keep the frequency discipline. |
| **FMyLife Email Technique** | Source site (fmylife.com) is a generic humor site with predominantly white/Western-coded scenarios. Afroyla's audience expects Black experience stories. Better alternative: mine customer-voices/ folder for real community stories. **Inferior + brand misfit.** |
| **Fiction Technique (Reality-Fiction Hybrid)** | This framework introduces clearly fictional scenarios into emails. Afroyla's brand trust is built on authentic real stories — customer quotes, Michelle's actual life, real cultural moments. Fictional escalation risks undermining authenticity with an audience that values genuine representation above all. Default = REJECT. Use only if Michelle explicitly frames something as creative fiction in the email itself. |
| **Foreign Slang Swipe File** | The framework recommends collecting "foreign slang" for entertainment and brand voice distinctiveness. Afroyla's brand language comes from Black American cultural vocabulary — AAVE, community language, Michelle's specific voice. Not wrong in principle; wrong framing for this brand. The underlying goal is already served by Afroyla's character universe. Skip this framework. |
