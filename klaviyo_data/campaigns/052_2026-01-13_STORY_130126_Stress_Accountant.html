This morning, 9:47 AM, Alice walks into my office holding a thick folder.

She doesn't look happy.

"Michelle," she says, tapping a spreadsheet with her pen. "We have that massive supplier payment due next week. And January cashflow is... well, it's January."

I nod, but my eyes stay glued to my screen.

I'm not looking at our bank account. I'm scrolling through Black Girls Code's website, reading about their 2026 programs.

Alice moves closer. Sees what's on my screen. Her eyes go wide.

"Michelle... is that a donation page?"

"Yup."

"Please tell me," she whispers, "you are not planning to give away money while I'm trying to figure out how to pay the factory."

I spin my chair around to face her.

"Alice. Business problems? We have them every month."

"Yes, and—"

"We always figure it out. We always do."

I turn my monitor toward her. Point at the photo of young Black girls hunched over laptops, coding.

"But THIS? This is bigger than a supplier invoice. This is about making sure when Nia grows up, she doesn't have to fight just to get a seat at the table."

Alice looks at the screen. Then at her spreadsheet. Then back at the screen.

Her jaw tightens.

"Michelle... I love Nia. You know I do. But we're talking about real money during our slowest month."

"I know."

"So—"

"Which is why I'm not asking the company to do it."

Alice frowns. "What do you mean?"

I pull up my personal bank account. Turn the screen toward her.

Her eyes scan the numbers. Then lock onto the scheduled transfer at the bottom: $1,000 to Black Girls Code. Payment date: January 20.

Alice blinks. Her shoulders drop.

"That's... your personal account."

"Yup. Next Tuesday, after MLK weekend is over, I'm donating $1,000. My money. Not Afroyla's."

Alice stares at the screen for a long moment. "Okay... that's generous, Michelle. But I still don't see how—"

"Because that's just the START," I say.

"Remember that MLK Weekend campaign I told you about yesterday?"

She nods slowly.

"I'm calling it The 200 Project."

I explain the math:

This weekend, any bag—any style, any color—will be 40-45% off.

For every order placed, Afroyla donates $5 to Black Girls Code.

"We need 200 orders," I say. "That's $1,000 from our community. Plus my $1,000. That's $2,000 total."

Alice picks up her pen. Starts scribbling numbers on the edge of her spreadsheet.

"200 orders... times average order value... minus the discount... plus margin..." She's muttering to herself now.

"By my rough calculation," I continue, "that could fund coding education for about 40 girls. Forty future Nias."

She looks up from her calculations. The "Finance Director" is still calculating margins. But the "Sister" is looking at those girls on my screen.

Finally, a small smile creeps onto her face.

"Well," she says, "if we hit 200 orders at 40% off, that DEFINITELY solves my cashflow problem."

She pauses. Taps her pen against the folder.

"And... my sister's birthday is next month. I've been eyeing that cognac tote." She looks up at me. "So make that 199 orders you need to find. I'm taking one."

I grin. "Deal."
